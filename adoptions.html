Adoptions, Parental Confirmations & Name Changes

Helping families navigate the legal process of building and defining their families.

View FAQs

Building a family takes many forms, and Ferguson Widmayer & Clark PC is honored to help clients through the legal processes that formalize these important relationships. We guide families through adoptions, parental confirmations, and name changes with care and attention to detail.

Adoption Services

Adoption is a profound way to build or expand your family. We assist with various types of adoptions, helping ensure the process is completed properly and efficiently under Michigan law.

  • Stepparent Adoptions: Formalizing the relationship between a stepparent and stepchild, including termination of parental rights when necessary.
  • Adult Adoptions: Creating a legal parent-child relationship between adults, often used to formalize a long-standing parental bond, establish inheritance rights, or recognize a stepparent relationship that was never legally formalized during childhood.
  • Consent & Termination: Handling consent requirements and termination of parental rights as required by law.
  • Court Representation: Representing families at adoption hearings and ensuring all legal requirements are satisfied.

Please note: Ferguson Widmayer & Clark does not handle agency, relative, private, or guardianship adoptions.

Parental Confirmations

Parental confirmation proceedings establish legal parentage for children, providing important legal protections and rights for both parents and children.

  • Parentage Confirmation: Legal proceedings to confirm parental rights, particularly important for unmarried parents, parents who conceive through IVF or surrogacy, and same-sex couples.
  • Birth Certificate Amendments: Obtaining corrected birth certificates to reflect legal parentage, including name and gender marker changes.

Name Changes

Whether you're changing your name after marriage, divorce, or for personal reasons, we help you navigate Michigan's legal name change process efficiently.

  • Adult Name Changes: Petitioning the court to legally change your name.
  • Minor Name Changes: Changing a child's name, including requirements for parental consent or notice.
  • Post-Adoption Name Changes: Updating a child's name following adoption.
  • Document Updates: Guidance on updating identification, records, and legal documents after a name change.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about adoptions and name changes in Michigan

The answers provided are for informational purposes only and do not constitute legal advice. Every situation is unique. Please schedule a consultation with one of our attorneys to discuss your specific circumstances.

The timeline varies by adoption type. Stepparent adoptions typically take 2-4 months once all paperwork is filed. Adult adoptions are often completed within 2-3 months. We work to move cases forward as efficiently as possible while ensuring all legal requirements are met.

Please note: Ferguson Widmayer & Clark does not handle agency, relative, private, or guardianship adoptions.

A stepparent adoption allows a stepparent to legally adopt their spouse's child, establishing a permanent parent-child relationship. Requirements include: marriage to the child's parent, the other biological parent's consent or termination of their rights, a home study may be required, and the stepparent must demonstrate fitness to parent. Once completed, the stepparent has all legal rights and responsibilities of a biological parent.

A parental confirmation (or order of parentage) legally establishes both same-sex parents as legal parents of their child. This is particularly important for the non-biological parent in situations involving assisted reproduction or surrogacy. The court order ensures both parents have full legal rights and protects the family if they travel to states with different laws or in the event of separation, medical emergencies, or death.

Adult name changes in Michigan require filing a petition with the probate court in your county of residence. You must provide a reason for the change, undergo a background check, and typically appear at a hearing. Publication in a local newspaper may be required. Once the court grants the name change, you'll receive a certified order to update your driver's license, Social Security card, and other documents. The process typically takes 4-8 weeks.

If you are building your family through IVF, surrogacy, or other assisted reproduction methods, comprehensive legal and estate planning is essential—and should not be left solely to donor clinic contracts or surrogacy agreements. Attorney Mara Kent at Ferguson Widmayer & Clark specializes in this area and can guide you through the following critical considerations:

Michigan's New Surrogacy Law & Parental Confirmation:

  • Michigan now legally permits gestational surrogacy agreements, providing a clearer path for intended parents
  • A parental confirmation proceeding establishes your legal parental rights, which is particularly important for same-sex couples, unmarried parents, and parents using donors or surrogates
  • This legal process ensures your name(s) appear on your child's birth certificate from the start

Termination of Donor or Gestational Carrier's Rights:

  • Proper legal documentation is required to terminate the parental rights of egg donors, sperm donors, or gestational carriers
  • Without this step, donors or carriers could theoretically assert parental claims—or be held liable for child support
  • Court orders provide permanent legal protection that clinic contracts alone cannot guarantee, and that may help best protect you if you travel to a state that may, one day, prohibit the type of family planning you choose

Planning for Incapacity or Death During the Process:

  • What happens if you become incapacitated while embryos are stored or a surrogate is pregnant?
  • Your power of attorney should specifically address decisions about assisted reproduction and stored genetic material
  • Your will or trust should include provisions for children conceived posthumously or born after your death
  • Consider naming guardians for children who may be born after your incapacity or death

Rights to Stored Genetic Material:

  • Who controls frozen embryos, eggs, or sperm if you divorce, become incapacitated, or die?
  • Estate planning documents should clearly state your wishes for stored genetic material
  • Consider whether you want embryos to be used, donated, destroyed, or preserved
  • Address inheritance rights for children conceived from stored material after your death

Why Clinic Contracts Are Not Enough:

  • Donor clinic and surrogacy agency contracts are primarily designed to protect the clinic or agency—and not necessarily you
  • These contracts may not address Michigan-specific legal requirements
  • Contracts generally cannot establish legal parentage—only a court order or birth certificate can do that
  • Agency contracts do not replace estate planning documents like wills, trusts, and powers of attorney

Comprehensive Estate Planning Integration:

  • Your will should include provisions for after-born or posthumously conceived children
  • Your trust should address how assets pass to children born through assisted reproduction
  • Powers of attorney should grant authority to make decisions about reproductive matters
  • Healthcare directives should address your wishes if decisions must be made while you're incapacitated

At Ferguson Widmayer & Clark, we understand the unique legal needs of families created through assisted reproduction. Contact us to ensure your family is fully protected.
