Probate, Estate Settlement & Trust Administration

Guiding families through the estate settlement process with sensitivity and efficiency.

View FAQs

Losing a loved one is difficult enough without the added burden of navigating complex legal proceedings. At Ferguson Widmayer & Clark PC, we guide families through probate and estate administration with compassion, clarity, and attention to detail.

Probate Administration

Probate is the legal process of settling a deceased person's estate under court supervision. Whether formal or informal proceedings are required, we help personal representatives fulfill their duties efficiently and in compliance with Michigan law.

  • Opening the Estate: Filing necessary petitions, publishing notice to creditors, and securing appointment of the personal representative.
  • Asset Inventory: Identifying, valuing, and documenting all estate assets.
  • Creditor Claims: Reviewing, negotiating, and paying valid claims against the estate.
  • Tax Compliance: Preparing and filing required estate, inheritance, and income tax returns.
  • Distribution: Transferring assets to beneficiaries in accordance with the will or state law.
  • Court Accounting: Preparing detailed accountings for approval of interested parties.

Trust Administration

When a loved one passes away with a trust, the successor trustee assumes significant responsibilities. We help trustees understand and fulfill their fiduciary duties while navigating the administration process.

  • Trustee Guidance: Explaining fiduciary duties, investment standards, and distribution requirements.
  • Asset Management: Assisting with retitling assets, managing investments, and maintaining proper records.
  • Beneficiary Communications: Preparing required notices and accountings for beneficiaries.
  • Tax Planning: Coordinating with tax professionals on trust income, estate, and generation-skipping taxes.
  • Distributions: Implementing distribution provisions according to trust terms.

Guardianship & Conservatorship

When individuals can no longer make decisions for themselves, guardianship or conservatorship may be necessary. We help families navigate these proceedings with sensitivity to the protected person's dignity and best interests.

  • Guardianship: Obtaining authority to make personal and medical decisions for incapacitated adults or minors.
  • Conservatorship: Securing authority to manage financial affairs for those who cannot do so themselves.
  • Ongoing Compliance: Preparing annual reports and accountings required by the court.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about probate and trust administration in Michigan

The answers provided are for informational purposes only and do not constitute legal advice. Every situation is unique. Please schedule a consultation with one of our attorneys to discuss your specific circumstances.

Probate is the court-supervised process of settling a deceased person's estate, including validating the will, paying debts, and distributing assets. In Michigan, probate typically takes 7-12 months, though complex estates may take longer. The timeline depends on factors such as estate size, whether there are disputes, and how quickly assets can be valued and distributed.

Yes, there are several ways to avoid probate in Michigan. Common strategies include creating and funding a revocable living trust; using transfer-on-death designations for financial accounts, securities, and business assets; naming beneficiaries on retirement accounts and life insurance policies; and joint ownership (however, we encourage you to consult with our attorneys first, as joint ownership can create unintended legal consequences). Proper estate planning can help most or all of your assets pass outside of probate.

A personal representative ["PR"] (called an executor or administrator in some states) is responsible for administering the probate estate (not to be confused with a Trustee who administers a Trust). The PR's duties include locating and securing assets, notifying creditors and beneficiaries, paying valid debts and taxes, managing estate property during administration, filing court documents and accountings, and ultimately distributing assets to beneficiaries according to the will or state law.

The total cost of probate will vary widely depending on the complexity of the estate. Very simple estates may be a few thousand dollars while more complex estates could be substantially more. The types of expenses include court fees, an inventory fee paid to the court based on the value of assets, legal fees, appraisals, accounting fees, taxes, bond premiums, and other potential expenses. Our attorneys can provide you with a more specific estimate based on your unique circumstances.

Trust administration is the process of managing and distributing trust assets after the Settlor's death. The Settlor [also known as a Grantor or Trustor in some states] is the person or people who created the trust. Unlike probate, trust administration is private, typically faster, and generally does not require court supervision. The successor trustee ["Trustee"] is the person who administers the trust after the Settlor/Initial Trustee has died, resigned, or becomes incapacitated. The Trustee has a fiduciary duty to follow the trust terms, notify beneficiaries, pay debts and taxes, and distribute assets. However, Trustees must still follow specific legal requirements and timelines as specified under the Michigan Trust code and the terms of the trust itself.
