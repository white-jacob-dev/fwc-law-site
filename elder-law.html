Elder Law

Protecting seniors and their families through comprehensive legal planning and advocacy.

View FAQs

As we age, legal needs become more complex. Ferguson Widmayer & Clark PC provides comprehensive elder law services to help seniors and their families navigate the challenges of aging with dignity, security, and peace of mind.

Long-Term Care Planning

Planning for potential long-term care needs is essential to protecting your assets and ensuring you receive quality care. We help families develop comprehensive strategies for funding and managing long-term care.

  • Medicaid Planning: Strategies to protect assets while qualifying for Medicaid benefits for nursing home or home care.
  • Asset Protection: Legal techniques to preserve assets for a healthy spouse or future generations.
  • Long-Term Care Insurance: Guidance on evaluating and coordinating long-term care insurance policies.
  • Veterans Benefits: Assistance with Aid and Attendance and other VA benefits for qualifying veterans and surviving spouses.
  • Care Coordination: Working with families to develop care plans that meet medical, financial, and personal needs.

Guardianship & Conservatorship

When a loved one can no longer make decisions for themselves, guardianship or conservatorship may become necessary. We guide families through these proceedings with sensitivity and respect for the protected person's dignity.

  • Guardianship: Court appointment to make personal, medical, and living arrangement decisions.
  • Conservatorship: Court appointment to manage financial affairs and protect assets.
  • Limited Guardianship: Tailored arrangements that preserve as much autonomy as possible.
  • Annual Reporting: Preparing required reports and accountings for the court.
  • Guardian Advocacy: Representing proposed guardians, family members, or the alleged incapacitated person.

Incapacity Planning

Proper incapacity planning can help avoid the need for guardianship and ensure your wishes are honored if you become unable to make decisions for yourself.

  • Durable Powers of Attorney: Designating trusted agents to handle financial and legal matters.
  • Patient Advocate Designations: Appointing someone to make medical decisions on your behalf.
  • HIPAA Authorizations: Allowing family members access to medical information.
  • Revocable Living Trusts: Providing for seamless management of assets during incapacity.

Elder Abuse & Exploitation

Sadly, seniors are often targets of abuse, neglect, and financial exploitation. We help families recognize warning signs and take action to protect vulnerable loved ones.

  • Financial Exploitation: Investigating and addressing theft, fraud, and undue influence.
  • Protective Orders: Obtaining court orders to protect seniors from abusers.
  • Recovery Actions: Pursuing legal remedies to recover misappropriated assets.
  • Facility Issues: Addressing concerns about care quality in nursing homes or assisted living facilities.

Special Needs Planning

For seniors with disabilities or those caring for disabled family members, special needs planning ensures continued eligibility for government benefits while providing for supplemental needs.

  • Special Needs Trusts: Providing for a disabled beneficiary without affecting government benefits.
  • ABLE Accounts: Tax-advantaged savings for individuals with disabilities.
  • Benefit Coordination: Ensuring inheritances and gifts don't disqualify beneficiaries from needed programs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about elder law in Michigan

The answers provided are for informational purposes only and do not constitute legal advice. Every situation is unique. Please schedule a consultation with one of our attorneys to discuss your specific circumstances.

Medicare is a federal health insurance program for people 65+ (and some younger people with disabilities). It covers hospital stays, doctor visits, and some skilled nursing care, but NOT long-term custodial care. Medicaid is a joint federal-state program for people with limited income and assets. In Michigan, Medicaid can pay for long-term nursing home care and some home care services. Many people use both programs together.

Medicaid planning strategies may include: irrevocable trusts established outside the 5-year look-back period, spousal protection rules that allow the healthy spouse to retain significant assets, converting countable assets to exempt assets (like paying off a mortgage), purchasing an annuity that provides income to a spouse, and Lady Bird deeds for real estate. Planning should begin as early as possible, but options exist even in crisis situations.

A Lady Bird deed (enhanced life estate deed) allows you to keep full control of your property during your lifetime—including the right to sell, mortgage, or revoke the deed—while automatically transferring the property to beneficiaries upon your death without probate. Importantly for Medicaid planning, the property passes outside the estate, potentially avoiding Medicaid estate recovery. It's a powerful tool for protecting the family home.

You can avoid the need for a guardianship or conservatorship with validly executed powers of attorney in most circumstances. However, a guardianship or conservatorship may be necessary when a loved one no longer has the mental capacity to sign legal documents or make safe decisions for themselves due to dementia, mental illness, developmental disability, or other conditions. Signs of potential incapacity include inability to manage finances, refusing necessary medical care, being exploited by others, or engaging in behavior that puts them at risk. Guardianships and conservatorships are court-supervised arrangements and should be considered a last resort after other, less restrictive options have been exhausted.

The ideal time to plan is 5+ years before you anticipate needing care, primarily because Medicaid has a 5-year "look-back" period for asset transfers. However, planning is valuable at any stage. Even if long-term care is imminent, there are crisis planning strategies available. If you're in good health, focus on long-term care insurance, powers of attorney, and revocable trusts. If care needs are approaching, consult with an elder law attorney about all available options.
